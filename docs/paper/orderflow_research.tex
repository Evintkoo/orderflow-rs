\begin{abstract}
\noindent
We present a systematic empirical study of order-flow and technical indicator
predictability at limit order book (LOB) snapshot frequency across 162
symbol--source combinations spanning foreign exchange, cryptocurrency, and
mixed-asset markets. Beginning with a validated synthetic LOB simulator
(Phase~P4), we establish ground-truth signal properties under controlled
conditions, then apply the same feature pipeline to 85 million real LOB
snapshots (Phase~P5). A walk-forward backtest (Phase~P6) quantifies the
gap between statistical predictability and net profitability under realistic
transaction costs. Finally (Phase~P7), we evaluate 87 classical and
microstructure-inspired technical indicators across four return horizons
(1\,s, 5\,s, 30\,s, 300\,s).

The principal findings are: (\emph{i})~cryptocurrency spot markets exhibit
strong sub-minute mean-reversion (Spearman IC$_{5\text{s}}=0.24$--$0.48$
for z-score and pivot-distance indicators); (\emph{ii})~FX spot markets show
near-zero predictability across all 87~indicators at $\sim$1\,s LOB
resolution, consistent with an efficient market-making regime; (\emph{iii})~
mean-reversion signals in crypto have a half-life well under 60\,s, as
demonstrated by a sign reversal when the same instruments are sampled via
REST~API (60\,s interval) rather than WebSocket (${\approx}0.3$\,s
interval); and (\emph{iv})~walk-forward OLS backtests on FX yield
uniformly negative net PnL at all tested cost thresholds, confirming that
the weak FX IC (0.02--0.04) is insufficient to overcome standard execution
costs.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

The predictability of short-horizon asset returns from limit order book (LOB)
information has been an active area of financial economics since the
availability of exchange-level depth-of-book data in the early 2000s.
Order-flow imbalance (OFI) --- the net signed pressure on the best quotes ---
has been shown by \citet{cont2014price} to predict returns at the
five-minute horizon with Spearman information coefficients (IC) of 0.15--0.25
in equity markets. Related work by \citet{zarinelli2015beyond} documents
impact decay and the role of order-book depth. In foreign exchange markets,
microstructure predictability has been documented at the daily horizon
\citep{evans2002order} but the intraday, sub-minute regime is less studied.

The cryptocurrency literature is more recent. \citet{bitmex2019} and
subsequent exchange-specific studies find stronger and more persistent
microstructure signals, plausibly because crypto markets attract a higher
share of retail order flow and lack the designated market-maker structures
prevalent in FX and equity markets.

\medskip

This paper contributes to the literature by providing the first systematic
\emph{cross-asset}, \emph{multi-source} evaluation of 87 technical indicators
at LOB-snapshot frequency, alongside a full simulation-to-backtest pipeline
implemented in the \code{orderflow-rs} research system. The scope covers:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
  \item \textbf{Phase P4} (\S\ref{sec:simulation}): A stochastic LOB
    simulator (Ornstein-Uhlenbeck price process, three-agent population,
    Hawkes arrivals) producing 2.88~million labeled rows per run.
  \item \textbf{Phase P5} (\S\ref{sec:p5}): Direct IC measurement on
    Dukascopy FX data across 10 pairs, testing five microstructure
    hypotheses.
  \item \textbf{Phase P6} (\S\ref{sec:backtest}): Walk-forward OLS backtest
    with realistic transaction costs on FX spot.
  \item \textbf{Phase P7} (\S\ref{sec:techanalysis}): Cross-asset technical
    indicator IC analysis, 162 instruments, 87 indicators, four horizons.
\end{itemize}

The remainder of the paper is organised as follows. Section~\ref{sec:data}
describes the data sources and preprocessing pipeline. Sections
\ref{sec:simulation}--\ref{sec:techanalysis} present the four phases.
Section~\ref{sec:discussion} synthesises the findings and discusses
implications for strategy design. Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes.

\subsection{Feature Construction}
\label{sec:features}

The LOB feature pipeline extracts 11 features per snapshot:

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{f}_t = \bigl[\OFI_1,\;\OFI_5,\;\OFI_{10},\;
  \delta_{\mathrm{depth}},\;\mu_{\mathrm{micro}},\;
  q_{\mathrm{imb}},\;\sigma_{\mathrm{spread}},\;
  \lambda_{\mathrm{trade}},\;\kappa_{\mathrm{impact}},\;
  \ell_{\mathrm{drain}},\;w_{\mathrm{slope}}\bigr]
\end{equation}

where $\OFI_k = \sum_{i=1}^{k}(\Delta Q^b_i - \Delta Q^a_i)$ is the
$k$-level order-flow imbalance, $\delta_{\mathrm{depth}}$ is depth
imbalance, $\mu_{\mathrm{micro}}$ is the microprice deviation from mid,
$q_{\mathrm{imb}}$ is queue imbalance, $\sigma_{\mathrm{spread}}$ is the
quoted spread, $\lambda_{\mathrm{trade}}$ is trade intensity, and the
remaining terms measure price impact, level drain, and weighted-mid slope.

Forward returns are computed at four non-overlapping horizons:
$r_{\tau} = \log(p_{t+\tau}/p_t)$ for $\tau \in \{1,5,30,300\}\,\text{s}$.

\subsection{Technical Indicator Construction}
\label{sec:techfeatures}

For the P7 analysis (Section~\ref{sec:techanalysis}), a normalised price
index is reconstructed from the 1-second return series:
\begin{equation}
  p_0 = 1.0,\qquad
  p_{i+1} = p_i \cdot \bigl(1 + 0.1\,r_{1\mathrm{s},i}\bigr)
\end{equation}
High and low approximations use the contemporaneous bid--ask spread
$\sigma_t$:
\begin{equation}
  h_t = p_t + \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma_t,\qquad
  l_t = \max\!\bigl(p_t - \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma_t,\;10^{-10}\bigr)
\end{equation}
Volume is proxied by trade intensity $\lambda_{\mathrm{trade}}$.
All 87 indicator functions are stateless
($\mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^n$) and operate on fixed-length prefix
arrays; the IC is computed only over the suffix where indicators are
defined.

\section{Phase P4: Synthetic LOB Simulation}
\label{sec:simulation}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\subsection{Simulation Design}
\label{sec:simdesign}

To validate the feature pipeline under controlled ground-truth conditions,
we implemented a full-day stochastic LOB simulator in Rust
(\code{src/simulator/lob\_sim.rs}). The price process follows an
Ornstein-Uhlenbeck (OU) model with jump component:
\begin{equation}
  dS_t = \kappa(\mu - S_t)\,dt + \sigma\,dW_t + J\,dN_t
\end{equation}
where $\kappa=0.1$\,s$^{-1}$, $\mu=100$, $\sigma=0.01/\sqrt{\text{s}}$,
$\lambda_J=0.02$\,jumps/s, $\sigma_J=0.05$. Prices are snapped to a
0.01-tick grid to ensure repeated level updates and non-zero OFI.

A three-agent population is simulated:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
  \item \textbf{Market Maker (3 instances):} Posts two-sided quotes at
    $\pm 2$ ticks from mid, refreshing every 0.5\,s. Stale quotes are
    cancelled before re-posting (no depth accumulation).
  \item \textbf{Noise Trader:} Hawkes-process arrivals ($\lambda_0=5$,
    $\alpha=0.3$, $\beta=1.0$), log-normal order sizes, symmetric
    buy/sell probability.
  \item \textbf{Informed Trader:} Poisson arrivals ($\lambda=0.5$/s),
    trades in the direction of the OU price deviation from long-run mean.
\end{itemize}

Ten independent paths of 8 hours each (28,800\,s) at 1\,ms time step
($10^7$ steps/path) were run, with LOB snapshots exported every 100\,ms,
yielding 2,880,000 labeled rows.

\subsection{Simulation Engineering Notes}
\label{sec:simbugs}

The following bugs were identified through unit testing and IC diagnosis:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
  \item \textbf{Mid-price vector truncation.} The \code{mid\_prices} vector
    was only pushed when \code{snap.mid().is\_some()}, causing a zip
    truncation to $\sim$36 rows/path. Fixed: use
    \code{snap.mid().unwrap\_or(s)} unconditionally.
  \item \textbf{Market orders adding instead of consuming.} Market orders
    used \code{apply\_delta} (adding to book) instead of
    \code{consume\_market\_order}, making OFI permanently zero. Fixed:
    implement a matching engine walk.
  \item \textbf{MM quotes at unique float prices.} No repeated level
    updates $\Rightarrow$ OFI $\equiv 0$. Fixed: tick-snap all prices to
    a 0.01 grid.
  \item \textbf{MM stale depth accumulation.} Old quotes were never
    cancelled. Fixed: track \code{mm\_active} bid/ask prices and cancel
    (qty$=0$ delta) before re-quoting.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Phase P6: Walk-Forward Backtest}
\label{sec:backtest}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\subsection{Methodology}
\label{sec:backtestmethod}

Phase~P6 evaluates whether the P5 statistical predictability translates into
net-positive trading performance under realistic costs. We use a
walk-forward OLS composite signal:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{r}_{t+\tau} = \hat{\beta}_0 + \sum_{j=1}^{5} \hat{\beta}_j f_{j,t}
\end{equation}
where $(f_1,\ldots,f_5) =
(\OFI_1,\,\delta_\mathrm{depth},\,\mu_\mathrm{micro},\,\sigma,\,
\ell_\mathrm{drain})$, trained on 14-day windows and tested on
7-day windows.

\paragraph{Critical design choices.}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
  \item \textbf{Non-overlapping bars:} Raw data at $\bar{\Delta t}=52\,\text{s}$
    with a 300\,s return horizon creates 83\% row-to-row overlap.
    Bars are resampled every $k=\text{round}(300/52)=6$ rows, yielding
    ${\approx}1{,}800$ non-overlapping test bars per window.
  \item \textbf{Liquidity filter:} Rows with spread $>5\times$ median
    spread are skipped; position is flat. This removes weekend and
    off-hours observations.
  \item \textbf{Execution costs:} Fixed fee $= 0.04\%$ per trade;
    half-spread cost $\approx 0.004\%$ (median spread
    $\approx 0.00008$).
\end{itemize}

Signal thresholds $k \in \{0.5, 1.5\}$ standard deviations are tested.

$-0.56$. Overlapping returns create spurious IC inflation of
$\approx5\times$ in backtest evaluation, underscoring the importance
of return-horizon alignment.

\subsection{IC Methodology}
\label{sec:icmeth}

Spearman rank IC is computed between each indicator series and the
corresponding forward return:
\begin{equation}
  \IC(x,y) = 1 - \frac{6\sum_{i=1}^{n} d_i^2}{n(n^2-1)},
  \qquad d_i = \mathrm{rank}(x_i) - \mathrm{rank}(y_i)
\end{equation}
A minimum of 10 paired non-null, finite observations is required.
The IC is set to \texttt{None} otherwise (occurs for indicators whose
warm-up period exceeds the available data length). Summary ICs in
all tables are averages across all symbol files with non-empty data
($n_\mathrm{files}=187$).

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\subsection{Why Is Crypto Predictable?}
\label{sec:discCrypto}

Four structural factors plausibly explain the strong technical IC in
cryptocurrency spot markets:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
  \item \textbf{Retail-dominated order flow.} Crypto markets attract a
    high share of price-chartist retail traders. Their systematic
    price-level reactions create the very patterns technical indicators
    are designed to detect.
  \item \textbf{No designated market makers.} Unlike FX dealer markets,
    crypto exchanges rely on algorithmic market makers who adjust
    slowly, leaving residual mean-reversion exploitable at LOB
    frequency.
  \item \textbf{Continuous 24/7 trading.} Absence of session gaps means
    rolling-window normalisation (z-score, BB) is not distorted by
    open/close jumps.
  \item \textbf{High absolute volatility.} Larger per-bar price moves
    increase the signal-to-noise ratio relative to transaction costs.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Why Is FX Unpredictable?}
\label{sec:discFX}

The near-zero IC across all 10 FX pairs despite $\sim$2.4M rows/pair is
striking. Competing explanations include:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
  \item \textbf{Efficient institutional market making.} FX spot prices
    are quoted by large banks with internalisation desks. Any
    systematic pattern is immediately arbitraged.
  \item \textbf{Return label noise.} At 1-second resolution in FX,
    the forward 5-second log-return may be below the noise floor set
    by the half-spread.
  \item \textbf{OHLC approximation limitations.} FX spreads are
    extremely tight ($\sim$0.1 pip for EUR/USD), so the high/low
    approximation via spread collapses to the mid-price series,
    reducing OHLC-based indicator diversity.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{The REST API Warning}
\label{sec:discREST}

The sign reversal between Binance WebSocket and REST data is the most
practically important result. It implies:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
  \item Mean-reversion signals at sub-minute horizons have a half-life
    $\ll 60\,\text{s}$.
  \item REST API data at 1-minute polling intervals is not merely
    underpowered --- it tests the wrong data-generating process
    (momentum or over-reversion rather than mean-reversion).
  \item \textbf{Any backtest using REST OHLCV data to study
    sub-minute strategies will obtain misleading, sign-reversed results.}
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Implications for Strategy Design}
\label{sec:strategy}

The findings suggest the following principles for sub-minute systematic
strategies:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
  \item \textbf{Target crypto, not FX.} The IC differential is an
    order of magnitude ($0.35$ vs.\ $0.00$). FX mean-reversion at
    this timescale requires either higher-frequency data or a
    fundamentally different signal class.
  \item \textbf{Use WS data infrastructure.} REST API polling
    invalidates the strategy directionally.
  \item \textbf{Z-score and pivot distance are the most reliable
    signals.} Both are simple, transparent, and consistently top-ranked
    across exchanges, instruments, and time horizons.
  \item \textbf{Walk-forward IC hurdle rate $\approx$ 0.10.} Based on
    P6 cost analysis extended to crypto fee structures ($\sim$0.04\%
    taker fee), an IC$_{5\text{s}} > 0.10$ OOS is the minimum threshold
    for positive expected net PnL.
  \item \textbf{Ignore plain moving averages.} SMA, EMA (all windows)
    carry IC$\approx 0$ at sub-minute frequency. Their inclusion in
    feature sets adds noise, not signal.
\end{itemize}

\section{Limitations and Future Work}
\label{sec:limitations}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\paragraph{Limitations.}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
  \item \textbf{No transaction cost adjustment for P7.} All IC values
    are gross. Net IC after fees and spread is lower.
  \item \textbf{Single time window.} Results may not generalise across
    different market regimes (e.g., low-volatility crypto bear markets).
  \item \textbf{Equity data unavailable.} Alpaca, Polygon, Finnhub,
    Tiingo sources produced empty feature files; equity microstructure
    predictability cannot be assessed.
  \item \textbf{OHLC approximation.} High/low prices are derived from
    spread, not observed tick data.
  \item \textbf{No multiple-comparison correction.} Testing 87 indicators
    simultaneously inflates Type-I error; results should be treated as
    hypothesis-generating, not confirmatory.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Future work (P8 and beyond).}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
  \item Walk-forward backtest applying the top-5 indicators to crypto
    WebSocket data with realistic exchange fees.
  \item Regime-conditional IC analysis: do z-score signals strengthen
    during high-volatility regimes?
  \item Multi-asset ensemble: combine FX OFI signals with crypto
    technical signals for a diversified portfolio.
  \item Machine learning extension: gradient-boosted trees on the 87
    indicator feature set with time-series cross-validation.
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

This paper presents the first systematic, cross-asset evaluation of
87 technical indicators at LOB snapshot frequency, embedded within a
four-phase pipeline covering simulation, statistical testing, walk-forward
backtesting, and information coefficient analysis.

The main conclusions are:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em,label=\textbf{C\arabic*.}]
  \item \textbf{Crypto mean-reversion is strong and consistent.}
    Z-score (10-bar), pivot distance, Bollinger \%B, Force Index, and
    RSI(7) achieve IC$_{5\text{s}}=0.15$--$0.48$ across 85 WebSocket
    crypto pairs, with monotonic power-law decay ($\alpha\approx 0.43$).
  \item \textbf{FX spot markets are technically efficient.}
    No technical indicator from any category (87~tested) is predictive
    in FX at $\sim$1\,s LOB resolution.
  \item \textbf{Data infrastructure determines signal direction.}
    WebSocket (0.3\,s) yields positive IC; REST (60\,s) yields negative
    IC for identical instruments. Signal half-life $\ll 60$\,s.
  \item \textbf{Trend-following is counter-productive at this timescale.}
    Moving averages and trend indicators show IC$\approx 0$ or negative.
  \item \textbf{P6 cost analysis provides the IC hurdle rate.}
    Even for the best FX pairs (IC$\approx 0.04$), net PnL is negative.
    Crypto IC values of 0.24--0.48 provide a substantially larger margin
    for profitable strategy design.
\end{enumerate}

The 34 indicators with $|\IC_{5\text{s}}|>0.05$ constitute a validated
candidate feature set for the next phase of research (P8): walk-forward
backtesting on cryptocurrency WebSocket data with full transaction costs.
